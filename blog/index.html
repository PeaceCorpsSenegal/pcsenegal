September 23, 2010 - 9:00 p.m.

On my way back from visiting the small village south of Keur Lahine Labe, in the region of Kaffrine, where Ousmane Willane, one of the master farmers from our food security program, was hosting an open field day. He showed off a number of productive techniques he has been trying with the encouragement and support of our staff and Volunteers.

This sort of work represents for me the best example of the kind of partnerships we are trying to promote: Volunteers working in teamwork with each other, with staff and with counterparts toward a common goal; agencies collaborating and learning from each other's activities, as demonstrated by the presence at the event by the other US government-funded food security including Wula Naafa, Projet Croissance Economique, and CLUSA; Senegalese counterparts learning from each other and building their capacity—more than 100 farmers from around area attended the open field day and learned from Ousmane's hard work and experimentation. I was able to give him a certificate marking his work in front of the whole community, helping to reinforce the societal value of skilled farmers.

It was also an opportunity to continue to build the network of highly skilled, very motivated master farmer, as the other 4 master farmers in the Peace Corps program from the Sine-Saloum attended Ousmane's event. We're hoping that exchange will engender some healthy learning, collaboration and maybe a bit of competition to everyone's benefit. Soon, we'll start to post detailed profiles of each of our master farmer's on the PC/Senegal web site, including video interviews, diagrams of their fields, and findings from their work. Thanks to all the Volunteers and staff whose efforts helped make the event such a success and a model to be replicated around the country.

September 17, 2010 - 6:00 p.m.

Just finished a three day workshop at Peace Corps Headquarters in Washington focused on helping the agency to develop an implementation strategy for the recommendations of the recent Peace Corps Comprehensive Assessment. This Assessment, mandated by Congress in last year's bill that appropriated the largest single year budget increase in Peace Corps history, outlined strategies on: how Peace Corps decides where to establish programs and leave countries; focusing our programming to maximize impact; improving technical training, especially given the reality that most Peace Corps Volunteers come with generalist backgrounds; using highly skilled Peace Corps Response Volunteers to help drive innovation; building partnerships to grow our "3rd Goal" efforts to bring the world back home; and improving Peace Corps operations through better use of technology and strong business practices.

We discussed and debated the priorities between and within these strategies and how best to turn them into reality. I was happy to have the opportunity to share some of the innovative approaches Peace Corps/Senegal Volunteers are promoting and to learn from what others are doing. In the end, I felt proud to be part of a group that was honestly and intensely trying to evaluate and fundamentally improve an institution about which we all care so much. I promise to keep you up to date and ask your advice as this process quickly unfolds. Here is a link to the text of the Comprehensive Assessment

September 2, 2010 - 8:00 p.m.

Spent the day visiting Trainees in their training villages. Until three years ago, most Peace Corps/Senegal pre-service training was conducted at our training center in Thies. Essentially we created an island of Americans surrounded by a sea of Senegal and tried to get them to learn language and culture in the classroom. Now, we have community-based training, in which most of the 9 weeks of training before they are sworn in is held in villages outside of Thies where 3-4 Trainees live with host families and are taught by a language and cultural facilitator, who also lives with a host family in the community. The Trainees come together to the training center every week or two for group technical, medical, safety and security briefings. Our language scores have gone way up since we moved to this model of immersion.

Returning from the Thies training center where I had the chance to talk with the group of trainees about the role of Peace Corps and PC Volunteers in development. August 16, 2010 – 1:00 p.m.

Today, the new group of Trainees heads off to their training villages to begin their experience of immersion in Senegalese language and culture. Since we adopted this “community-based training” model, Trainee language capabilities have improved dramatically and we have raised our minimum local language proficiency standard at the end of the 9-week pre-service training from Intermediate-Low to Intermediate-Mid. Fully half of the last group actually achieved Intermediate-High or Advanced in languages such as Wolof, Pulaar, Mandinka and Sereer before being sent off to their Volunteer sites. This communication ability, and integration into the host communities that it facilitates, is a key part of the foundation for successful service, and an attribute that sets PC Volunteers apart from other development workers.
